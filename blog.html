Heritage Under Water: Introduction, Inspiration & Significance Behind the Project

Oct 30, 2017


For our second portfolio project, we were asked to create a project inspired by the theme “The Bones of Our Past”. Initially, I wanted to create something that was personalized to my family and ancestry. I brainstormed with my Dad and we decided it would be neat to create a digital narrative of the ship that my Great-Grandfather fought on during the First World War. My Great Grandfather, John Douglas Chater, was deployed on a ship called HMS Flirt. On the 27th of October, 1916, while the ship was attempting to stop the Germans from advancing further into the seas of Dover, the crew came across a wreckage where another British ship had been mined. My Great-Grandfather and 2 other men hopped into a safety boat to rescue some of the survivors out of the freezing Atlantic. While they were in the water, HMS flirt was torpedoed by Germans. The ship went down in 8 minutes and John Chater along with 5 other men were the only survivors out of the 60 person crew.

The information that I found on HMS Flirt was not sufficient enough to support an entire project, so I thought it would be best to broaden my topic. I decided to research ship wreckages in England that are from the First World War. Due to the fact that there are hundreds of wreckages, for the sake of this project, I narrowed it down to the WW1 warships of Dover that sunk between the years of 1914-1918. Even more specifically, the ships that are frequently explored by and available to divers. (Note: I did not include submarines or shipwrecks that did not belong to the British Navy). This project uses a digital map to show the approximate location of 13 war-shipwrecks in the seas of Dover, as well as a brief summary of their story.

I feel that this concept relates perfectly to the theme of this course as it represents exactly how digital advances allow anthropologists to explore and further understand archaeological sites that would otherwise be near impossible to do without technology. Due to the fact that underwater wreckages are not visible or accessible to many, they are often overlooked. I feel that shipwrecks should be analyzed more heavily as they are the burial sites of millions of people and they are a source of so much information about human past.

The Shipwreck Locations off the Shore of Dover




It was very difficult to find a website that would openly share the locations of shipwrecks with the public. I assume that this secrecy is not only to keep the wrecks as undisturbed & respected burial sites, but also to inhibit the ability of people plundering the wrecks and selling their findings online. After a long search, I finally found a site that provided the approximate coordinates of all shipwrecks around the UK. This is the site that I used for the coordinates!

I used the Canterbury Divers Site to discover the shipwrecks that have previously been and are currently able to be explored. The brief descriptions provided were also derived from this site. Canterbury Divers Source!

Reflection


Although at first the broad topic of made this project seem intimidating, once I settled on a general idea, I definetly became more interested in the mapping concept than I anticipated. I for sure have run into numerous hiccups but overall I think the project turned out okay! To start, narrowing down a topic was deffinetly a struggle for me. I knew I wanted to do something where I could encorporate the story of one of my relatives but all the information I was finding was too scarce to create a project off of. With each idea I found that I would either have too many data-points or too little. Eventually, I decided on a rather specific theme as it allowed my study to remain organized but I still was able to stick with the topic of shipwreaks. As I mentioned above, finding a site that provided the coordinates of shipwrecks was also an aspect of this topic that required me to do a lot of digging as it is not something that is made easily accesible to the general public, for obvious reasons. I also had difficulty finding photos of the ships that I was allowed to use on my site (this explains the lack of photo use). When starting to input my information into Carto, I couldn't seem to figure out how to add numerous points in one layer. This was obviously an insanely easy fix, but it did resut in me having to re-input all my datasets and was a minor set back as I didn't want to advance until I knew that I was doing it the right way. Once I got the hang of it the Carto process was a breeze I'm happy with how it turned out. lastly, trying to input everything onto my website was also a big learning curve, but one that I think is innevitable when you are as technoloically challenged as myself. My goal for some time in the next coming weeks is to figure out how to make my blog and portfolio two seperate page because I just couldn't quite seem to figure that one out. Other than a few content and technical difficulties, I really enjoyed reading the various artices about how digital archarology can be used to recover such interesting and eerie sites that don't normally get much attention.
